Brief

Background

TransCanWork (TCW) is a nonprofit organization that provides job-hunting resources and support for trans, gender-diverse, and intersex (TGI) jobseekers. They also create safer and more comfortable work environments from the ground up by offering training and certification to employers, equipping them with the tools they need to help improve TGI representation in the professional sphere.

Team

For the creation of this web application, I worked alongside a wonderful team consisting of 1 product manager (Nasser), 2 fellow designers (Jiyeon, Megan), and 10 developers (Aaron, Alan, Angela, Dan, Diya, Jiamin, Kaylee, Kevin, Ryan, Solia).

Problem

The workflow TCW used for keeping track of next steps for jobseekers was simply a Google Sheets document. For navigators (professionals assigned to jobseekers to guide them along their journey), repeating this process for many different clients was cumbersome and susceptible to human error. For jobseekers, the process was not very interactive or transparent—jobseekers had no way of even seeing their own progress!

As the organization grew, this manual process became less manageable and desirable for both navigators and jobseekers.

How might we represent the jobseeking process in a standardized, yet fun and interactive way?

Goal

CREATE A DELIGHTFUL CLIENT-SIDE EXPERIENCE.

In order to maintain engagement and motivation in what can be a demoralizing process, we wanted to make the external-facing app for jobseekers as engaging and visually striking as possible.

FOCUS ON ACCESSIBILITY AND EASE-OF-USE.

We wanted the website to be an easily navigable and accessible way for jobseekers to track their progress along the jobseeking process. Simultaneously, we also sought to structure content in a way that would make internal management simple for navigators.

PRIORITIZE LONG-TERM USABILITY AND UPKEEP.

Since TCW doesn’t have any dedicated developers or designers on staff, designing a flexible and thoroughly fleshed out product was critical to ensuring the app’s long-term usefulness.

Research

Competitive Analysis

We conducted an analysis of three competitors which offer services similar to that of TCW’s: JibberJobber, Youth@Work, and Job Search Journey. We were then able to identify commonalities between the sites which suggested features we should incorporate in our own product, as well as things we noticed that we'd want to avoid doing.

Contextual Inquiry

Next, my team and I interviewed 2 TransCanWork navigators to better understand the issues they were facing. These conversations confirmed from firsthand sources that TCW’s process was highly manual, and required much individual work and organization from the navigators’ part to keep track of all of TCW’s jobseekers.

The current process works, but it’s pretty limited… it’s very cluttered and spread out because there’s an overload of info.

— TCW Navigator

Key Takeaways

TCW’S SYSTEMS WERE UNEQUIPPED TO HANDLE LARGE NUMBERS OF CLIENTS.

While TCW’s spreadsheets were the very backbone of their work, they also contributed to a slow, tedious workflow since each jobseeker had their own individual spreadsheet. Our research revealed that navigators could expect to be responsible for up to 100 jobseekers at a time—imagine the pain of managing 100 spreadsheets full of client info!

JOBSEEKERS FELT REMOVED FROM THEIR OWN JOB-SEARCHING PROCESS.

During our inquiries with TCW navigators, we found out that jobseekers only get to meet with their navigators once every 2 weeks. Between each session, jobseekers had no way of accessing the data—about their own job-searching progress—that navigators were seeing. To make matters worse, they lacked easy communication with their navigators, leading to an uninteractive and isolating experience.

Process

User Flow

My team began by mapping out a simple user flow based on the three types of TCW users: administrators, navigators, and jobseekers (my main population of interest for this project). This grounded us with a home base from which we could ideate possible solutions from the perspectives of all kinds of TCW members.

Initial Sketches

My team—in collaboration with the lovely folks over at TCW who acted as our liasions—decided to represent jobseeker milestones in the form of a pirate roadmap! This was inspired by Our Flag Means Death, an HBO rom-com about gay pirates that had just released.

From this point on, I assumed full ownership over this portion of the project. I began by creating a (very) rudimentary sketch, incorporating my idea to make a fun, alliterative name for each island!

Iterations

After making my low-fidelity sketches, one of the first things to do was to replace the blobs with actual islands! Each of the 10 islands represented a section of the spreadsheet that TCW used to use to manage jobseekers. Since this would be a client-facing interface, my main goal here was to create a unique visual theme and name for each one.

Fast forward several more iterations (I wish I could show you the whole process!)… the next major update came after recieving some preliminary feedback from TCW. I rearranged the islands into a more intuitive order, and adjusted the roadmap details popup to align with TCW’s style guide. In addition, I made some more stylistic adjustments to add visual interest!

Usability Testing

At this point, the app was ready for testing with some real users. My team recruited 3 participants—a mix of jobseekers and navigators—to try out the new roadmap. We received some valuable feedback in the process!

Remove repetitive elements.

The Menu button on the topleft, which served solely as an alternative way of navigating the islands, was only complicating things without providing any additional accessibility. After some discussion, my team and I opted to replace it with a link to an external calendar of TCW events, for easy jobseeker access.

DESIGN FOR DEVICES OF ALL KINDS.

Even in our relatively small testing pool, we saw participants joining the call from tablets and mobile phones. Certain parts of our prototype weren’t equipped to handle these touch interactions, which only emphasized that we couldn’t bank on every user accessing TCW through a computer (an assumption we'd previously made).

Final Designs

Employment Roadmap

After what feels like countless iterations: the final employment roadmap! To help guide jobseekers along their journey, the dotted line to the red X (in keeping with the pirate theme) provides a sense of direction. Islands will also start black-and-white, then fill in with color once their corresponding milestone is completed, so that progress feels more rewarding and interactive.

Roadmap Details

Clicking or tapping on an island reveals a popup with details about the milestone it represents. They can include forms to fill out, checklists to mull over, resources to check out, and more! These popups allow jobseekers to easily see and access tasks without having to wait for their next navigator check-in. As requested by TCW, bits of pirate lingo are also sprinkled throughout!

Profile Page

For the first time, TCW jobseekers can create profiles to show off their personality. Full profile customization, including avatar and banner image selection, provides jobseekers control over their job-hunting voyage. The profile page also acts as a way to easily reach your assigned navigator, if the need arises.

Responsive Design

Jobseekers can also access their roadmaps from the convenience of their phones! The roadmap responsively condenses into a linear, scrollable design to take full advantage of smaller screens while still providing the feeling of navigating a map.

Prototype Demo

Style Guide

For a project of this size, creating an organized style guide was crucial. Display fonts and full custom color palettes for each island were meticulously chosen for the most visually appealing and pirate-like experience possible. And it's not just for show: this style guide also expedited design speed in Figma by an estimated 75%!

Reflections

DESIGN CAN TAKE MANY FORMS.

When I was first breaking into design, I never thought I’d one day be creating a pirate map for job-hunting. This project, with all its flashy colors and illustrations, was much different from the minimalistic designs I was used to.

However, doing something a bit outside of my design comfort zone was incredibly refreshing and eye-opening. I got to create a gamified client interface—a rare opportunity—and I was also introduced a whole world of maximalist design trends and styles that I had yet to previously delve into. I cannot wait to see what else is out their in the world of design!

Keep an Organized Design File.

This project helped me realize that in many ways, design is more about working smart than working hard (although I'd like to think I am doing both!) This can mean maintaining organized design files that make full use of components. Componentizing everything from input fields to decorational shipwrecks ended up saving me hours of time, allowing for a more quality end result.

My only regret is that I wish I’d started sooner—I only bit the bullet and componentized all of my work around the third iteration—but now I know better for whatever project I take on next!

... And a big thank you to the TransCanWork team for holding the most wonderfully chaotic work sessions, for implementing all the crazy designs I came up with without a complaint, and for always being up for a discussion about whether Helen Keller really flew a plane.
